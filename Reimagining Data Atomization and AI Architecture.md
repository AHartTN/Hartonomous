This document serves as the comprehensive architectural specification for the Hartonomous system, correcting previous logical errors regarding indexing versus semantics and enforcing the strict separation of "Constants" and "Compositions."

### **1.1 The Bifurcation of Compute and Storage**

The fundamental flaw in the modern AI stack is the bifurcation of compute and storage. In a standard Deep Learning workflow, the "intelligence" resides in the GPU's VRAM (Volatile Random Access Memory) in the form of model weights, while the "knowledge" (the dataset) resides in a data lake or SQL database. These two worlds are bridgeable only by massive data pipelines and training runs. The database is a dumb container; the model is a volatile savant.  
The Hartonomous architecture rejects this duality. It posits that the persistence layer *is* the cognitive layer. ### **1.2 The Spatial Realignment**

The core innovation of Hartonomous is the translation of semantic meaning into spatial geometry. **2\. Universal Atomization: The Ontology of Existence**

The foundational principle of the Hartonomous architecture is **Universal Atomization**. This is the strict enforcement of a granular ontology that forbids the existence of composite data types within the storage layer's fundamental units. In traditional Relational Database Management Systems (RDBMS) or modern Vector Databases, optimization is sought through aggregation—compressing data into columns of arrays, JSON blobs, or dense binary vectors to minimize row counts. Hartonomous explicitly forbids this. Intelligence arises from the *relationships* between fundamental units, not from the compression of those units into opaque formats.2

### **2.1 The Two Classes of Existence**

The universe of Hartonomous is strictly binary. Every entity that exists is either a **Constant** or a **Composition**. This dichotomy is not merely a classification; it is a structural constraint that dictates storage strategy, identity generation, and spatial behavior.

#### **2.1.1 The Constant: The Finite Substrate**

A **Constant** is an atomic unit of meaning that cannot be subdivided. It is the fundamental particle of the cognitive universe. Crucially, and distinct from previous architectural iterations, the set of Constants is **finite** and **limited**.  
In a continuous floating-point universe, the number of unique values is effectively infinite. Storing every unique float as an Atom would bloat the index with noise and fragment the semantic space. Therefore, Hartonomous imposes strict quantization.

* **Quantization Imperative**: A raw input value, such as a pixel brightness of 0.751239 or an audio frequency, is quantized to a limited set of values (e.g., 256 discrete levels for 8-bit depth).  
* **The Limited Set**: The system is initialized with a "Standard Model" of Constants. This includes:  
  * **Numeric Constants**: A fixed set of quantized numbers (e.g., integers \-1000 to 1000, and quantized floats with 0.01 precision).  
  * **Token Constants**: A fixed vocabulary of characters (ASCII/Unicode) or sub-word tokens (akin to the cl100k\_base vocabulary).  
  * **Sensory Constants**: Discrete values for sensory inputs (e.g., the 16,777,216 distinct colors of the 24-bit RGB space, or a reduced palette of 256 colors).

By limiting the set of Constants, we ensure that the "ground" of the semantic space is stable. The Atom for the value 0.5 exists exactly once. Every concept in the universe that relies on the weight or value 0.5 must reference this single, immutable geometric point. This solves the "Curse of Dimensionality" by anchoring high-dimensional concepts to a low-dimensional, finite set of anchors.2

#### **2.1.2 The Composition: The Infinite Web**

A **Composition** is a relationship between Atoms. If Constants are the nodes, Compositions are the edges—or more accurately, the *paths*. A Composition represents a thought, a sequence, a vector, an object, or a narrative.

* **Geometric Representation**: Compositions are strictly stored as LINESTRING ZM geometries. A Composition is defined not by what it *is*, but by the trajectory it traces between its constituent Atoms.  
* **Recursive Nature**: A Composition can connect Constants (e.g., a "Word" connecting "Letter" Constants) or it can connect other Compositions (e.g., a "Sentence" connecting "Word" Compositions).  
* **Infinite Potential**: While the set of Constants is finite (e.g., 256 values), the number of valid Compositions is combinatorially infinite. However, due to the **Law of Identity**, any specific combination exists exactly once.

### **2.2 Structured Deterministic Identity (SDI)**

In legacy systems, identity is often an afterthought—a surrogate key, such as an auto-incrementing integer or a random UUID, assigned at the moment of insertion. This practice decouples the identity of a datum from its content, leading to semantic drift, duplication, and the inability to recognize the recurrence of identical concepts across different contexts.1  
The Hartonomous system enforces **Structured Deterministic Identity (SDI)** as the absolute mechanism for existence.

| Feature | Legacy Identity (UUID/Serial) | Structured Deterministic Identity (SDI) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Generation** | Random or Sequential | Cryptographic Hash of Content |
| **Collision** | statistically improbable (UUID) | Guaranteed for identical content |
| **Deduplication** | Requires lookup/index scan | Automatic (Mathematical) |
| **Reference** | Central Coordination required | Decentralized / Content Addressable |
| **Persistence** | Mutable | Immutable |

#### **2.2.1 The Hashing Mechanism**

The mechanism for SDI is a cryptographic hash of the canonicalized data payload. We utilize **BLAKE3** or **SHA-256** to generate a 32-byte binary hash (BYTEA) for every Atom.

* **For Constants**: $ID \= \\text{Hash}(\\text{Type} \+ \\text{Value})$.  
  * Example: SHA256("FLOAT" \+ "0.75").  
* **For Compositions**: $ID \= \\text{Hash}(\\text{Concatenation of Child IDs})$.  
  * Example: If Atom $C$ is a composition of Atoms $A$ and $B$, then $ID\_C \= \\text{SHA256}(ID\_A \+ ID\_B)$.

This creates a Merkle-DAG (Directed Acyclic Graph) structure. The implications are profound:

1. **Automatic Deduplication**: If two distinct ingestion pipelines process the same file or concept, they will independently generate the exact same Hash ID. When they attempt to insert this Atom into the database, the second insertion will trigger an ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING clause. The database does not store a duplicate; it merely reinforces the existence of the original.  
2. **Universal Addressability**: Any component of the system can generate the address of a concept if it knows the concept's content. This enables decentralized reference resolution without querying a master index.  
3. **Immutability**: An Atom cannot change. If the content changes, the hash changes, and thus a new Atom is born. This creates an append-only universe of thought, where "updates" are actually the creation of new truths and the potential decay of old relationships.1

**3\. The Ingestion Architecture: The External Shader Pipeline**

A critical requirement of the Hartonomous architecture is that the database must remain a pristine engine of storage and retrieval. It must not be bogged down by the CPU-intensive tasks of parsing, hashing, or algorithmic calculation. Therefore, **Ingestion** is decoupled into an external high-performance service known as the **Shader Pipeline**.  
The Shader is a compiled application (written in C++ or Rust) that sits between the raw data streams and the database. It is responsible for transforming raw noise into structured geometry before the data ever touches the SQL boundary.

### **3.1 Step 1: Quantization and Tokenization**

The raw stream (text, video, audio, logs) enters the Shader. The first step is to map this continuous or high-entropy stream onto the "Limited Set" of Constants.

* **For Text**: The Shader applies a tokenizer (e.g., BPE) to break the text into tokens. Each token is mapped to its corresponding Constant Atom ID.  
* **For Numerics**: Floating point numbers are rounded to the system's quantization precision (e.g., 2 decimal places).  
* **For Images**: Pixels are quantized to their nearest color palette match.

This step ensures that the infinite variability of the real world is collapsed into the finite vocabulary of the Hartonomous system.

### **3.2 Step 2: Run-Length Encoding (RLE)**

Data streams are often repetitive. Storing every signal is inefficient and semantically weak. "Intelligence" is found in the changes, the edges, and the structure, not in the void. Therefore, the Shader applies **Run-Length Encoding (RLE)** as a universal filter.2

* **The Mechanism**: The Shader iterates through the stream of quantized constants. It collapses consecutive identical signals into tuples of (Value, RunLength).  
  * Input: A, A, A, A, B, B  
  * RLE Output: (A, 4), (B, 2\)  
* **Geometric Translation**: This is where Time becomes Space. The "Run Length" is not stored as a number in a column; it is encoded into the **M (Measure)** dimension of the geometry.  
  * The transition A \-\> B becomes a segment of a LINESTRING ZM.  
  * Vertex A: POINT(..., M=4)  
  * Vertex B: POINT(..., M=2)  
* **Insight**: This transforms "Dwell Time" (how long the system attended to a signal) into a geometric weight. A path segment with high $M$ values represents a "thick" synaptic connection—a relationship that the system has "dwelled" upon for a long time. It signifies a strong, ingrained association. A path with low $M$ is a fleeting, tentative connection.

### **3.3 Step 3: Constant Pair Encoding (CPE) and Recursive Composition**

To build the Z-axis (Hierarchy) of the semantic space, the Shader applies **Constant Pair Encoding (CPE)**. This is a recursive algorithm similar to Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) or Sequitur, but applied to the stream of Atoms.

1. **Frequency Analysis**: The Shader buffers a window of the incoming stream. It identifies the most frequent adjacent pair of Atoms (e.g., Constants "H" and "a").  
2. **Merge**: It creates a new Composition Atom "Ha" representing this sequence.  
3. **Recursive Check**: It checks if "Ha" combined with the next atom "r" forms a known higher-level Composition "Har".  
4. **Geometry Calculation**: The geometry of the new Composition is derived (initially) as the weighted barycenter of its parents. The Z-coordinate is incremented: $Z\_{\\text{new}} \= \\max(Z\_{\\text{left}}, Z\_{\\text{right}}) \+ 1$.

This process happens entirely in the Shader's memory. The database only receives the final, fully composed structures. This offloads the $O(N^2)$ complexity of pair discovery from the database kernel.2

### **3.4 Step 4: The Hilbert Index Calculation (Cold Start)**

A major correction in this architectural revision is the handling of the **Hilbert Index**. In previous iterations, there was ambiguity between the Semantic Geometry and the Storage Index. Hartonomous strictly separates them.

* **The Problem**: Spatial data (Geometry) is multidimensional and hard to order on a linear disk. Random insertion leads to index fragmentation and random I/O, killing performance.  
* **The Solution**: We order the data on disk using a **Hilbert Space-Filling Curve**.  
* **External Calculation**: Calculating the Hilbert index for a high-dimensional vector is CPU intensive. The Shader performs this calculation *before* the data is sent to the DB.  
  * **Input**: The raw static features of the Atom (e.g., the byte distribution of the token, or the RGB values of the color).  
  * **Output**: A 64-bit integer (hilbert\_idx).  
* **Purpose**: This index is used **strictly** for physical clustering (CLUSTER USING). It ensures that Atoms with similar raw characteristics (which are likely to be queried together during cold starts) are written to the same physical disk pages. It is the "Library Shelf Number," not the "Meaning of the Book".1

### **3.5 Step 5: Bulk Loading via COPY Protocol**

Inserting millions of atoms row-by-row (INSERT INTO) is too slow due to transaction overhead and SQL parsing. The Shader utilizes the **PostgreSQL COPY Protocol**.

* **Binary Stream**: The Shader constructs a binary stream matching the PostgreSQL internal tuple format.  
* **Direct Heap Access**: The COPY command (COPY atoms FROM STDIN (FORMAT BINARY)) parses the stream and writes directly to the table heap, bypassing much of the query executor overhead.  
* **Transaction Management**: The Shader batches atoms (e.g., 10,000 at a time) into single transactions. This ensures ACID compliance while maximizing throughput.  
* **Conflict Resolution**: Since COPY typically aborts on duplicate primary keys (and our SDI guarantees duplicates will happen), the Shader employs a "Stage-and-Merge" strategy:  
  1. COPY data into a temporary staging table (no indexes, super fast).  
  2. Execute a single SQL command to merge the staging table into the main atoms table:  
     SQL  
     INSERT INTO atoms SELECT \* FROM staging ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING;

  3. Truncate staging.

This architecture ensures that the database acts purely as a sink for pre-processed, pre-indexed, and pre-optimized data.

**4\. The Database Substrate: Schema and Storage**

The "Brain" of the Hartonomous system is a PostgreSQL database augmented with the **PostGIS** extension. The schema is designed to leverage the internal page structure of PostgreSQL for maximum locality and scan speed.

### **4.1 The Single-Table Design**

The architecture utilizes a single, massive table named atoms. This unifies the address space, allowing Compositions to reference Constants and other Compositions without cross-table joins. This supports the "Universal Atomization" philosophy: everything is an atom, so everything belongs in the atom table.

#### **4.1.1 Table Definition**

The following SQL definition illustrates the rigorous implementation of the schema.

SQL

\-- Enable the Spatial Substrate  
CREATE EXTENSION IF NOT EXISTS postgis;

\-- Define the fundamental duality of existence  
CREATE TYPE atom\_class AS ENUM ('constant', 'composition');

\-- The Unified Persistence Layer  
CREATE TABLE atoms (  
    \-- IDENTITY: The Anchor  
    \-- The deterministic hash (SDI). Stored as BYTEA for space efficiency (32 bytes).  
    \-- Acts as the Primary Key.  
    sdi\_id BYTEA NOT NULL,

    \-- PHYSICS: The Meaning  
    \-- X, Y: Semantic Manifold Coordinates (Derived via LMDS).  
    \-- Z: Hierarchical Depth / Abstraction Level.  
    \-- M: Measure / Global Salience / Sequence Index.  
    \-- Geometry is 4D: PointZM or LineStringZM.  
    geom GEOMETRY(GEOMETRYZM, 4326\) NOT NULL,

    \-- STORAGE: The Physical Location  
    \-- The Hilbert Curve Index. Strictly for B-Tree clustering and partitioning.  
    \-- This ensures disk locality for cold starts.  
    hilbert\_idx BIGINT NOT NULL,

    \-- METADATA & CLASSIFICATION  
    class atom\_class NOT NULL,  
      
    \-- PAYLOAD  
    \-- The raw data (e.g., the string 'Apple' or the serialized composition metadata).  
    \-- Stored EXTERNAL (TOAST) to keep the main B-Tree lean and scan speeds high.  
    data BYTEA,

    \-- TEMPORAL METADATA  
    created\_at TIMESTAMPTZ DEFAULT NOW(),  
    last\_accessed TIMESTAMPTZ DEFAULT NOW(),

    CONSTRAINT pk\_atoms PRIMARY KEY (sdi\_id)  
) PARTITION BY RANGE (hilbert\_idx);

### **4.2 Storage Strategy: Separation of Index and Meaning**

A critical correction from previous architectures is the explicit separation of the **Storage Index** from the **Semantic Value**.

| Feature | Hilbert Index (hilbert\_idx) | Semantic Geometry (geom) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Source** | Calculated from raw static features (bytes) | Calculated from dynamic relationships (context) |
| **Stability** | Static (never changes) | Dynamic (evolves with learning) |
| **Index Type** | B-Tree (1D) | GiST (R-Tree, 4D) |
| **Purpose** | **Physical Placement** (Disk Locality) | **Logical Inference** (Similarity Search) |
| **Query Use** | Partition Pruning, Initial Lookups | ST\_DWithin, ST\_Intersects, Traversal |

#### **4.2.1 Physical Clustering**

We utilize the hilbert\_idx to cluster the table physically.

SQL

CLUSTER atoms USING idx\_atoms\_hilbert;

This command rewrites the table heap, ordering rows based on the Hilbert index. Because the Hilbert curve preserves locality, Atoms that are similar in their raw form (e.g., "Color 100" and "Color 101") will be placed on the same 8KB disk page. This minimizes disk seek latency when loading related groups of atoms.1

#### **4.2.2 Partitioning**

The table is **Range Partitioned** using the hilbert\_idx. This allows us to split the "Brain" across multiple physical disks or tablespaces. For example, "Visual Atoms" (which might cluster in a specific range of the Hilbert curve) can be stored on a separate NVMe drive from "Textual Atoms."

### **4.3 The TOAST Strategy for Payloads**

The data column, which stores the actual content (e.g., the text "Apple"), is stored using PostgreSQL's **TOAST** (The Oversized-Attribute Storage Technique) mechanism. We configure the storage strategy to EXTERNAL.

* **Logic**: We rarely need to read the raw text during the inference process (which is purely geometric). By forcing the data out-of-line, we keep the main table rows very small (just IDs and Geometries).  
* **Benefit**: This packs more Atoms per disk page, drastically increasing the cache hit ratio for the spatial index and accelerating geometric traversals.1

**5\. The Laws of Physics: The C++ Cortex**

Once data is stored, it is effectively "dead" unless organized. In a neural network, organization happens during the backward pass (Backpropagation). In Hartonomous, organization is a continuous background process governed by the **Cortex**.  
The **Cortex** is implemented as a set of PostgreSQL **Background Workers** (worker\_spi) written in C++. It acts as the "Physics Engine" of the database, enforcing the laws of geometry on the stored Atoms. It bypasses the SQL parser for internal logic, accessing the graph data directly via the Server Programming Interface (SPI) or low-level heap access for maximum speed.1

### **5.1 Law 1: Landmark Multidimensional Scaling (LMDS)**

The Cortex must solve the problem of embedding high-dimensional relationships (co-occurrences in Compositions) into the low-dimensional (2D/3D) space of the geom column. Classical Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) requires the eigendecomposition of an $N \\times N$ distance matrix, an operation with $O(N^3)$ complexity. For a database with billions of atoms, this is impossible.  
Hartonomous employs **Landmark MDS (LMDS)**, which reduces the complexity to $O(k \\cdot N)$, where $k$ is a small number of "Landmark" points ($k \\ll N$).1

#### **5.1.1 The MaxMin Landmark Selection**

The integrity of the projection depends on the quality of the landmarks. The Cortex employs the **MaxMin** strategy to ensure the landmarks cover the convex hull of the semantic space.

1. **Initialization**: Select the first landmark $L\_1$ randomly from the Atoms table.  
2. **Iteration**: For $i \= 2$ to $k$:  
   * Calculate the distance from all candidate atoms to the existing set of landmarks $\\{L\_1, \\dots, L\_{i-1}\\}$.  
   * Select the atom that maximizes the *minimum* distance to the existing set.  
   * $L\_i \= \\text{argmax}\_{a \\in A} (\\min\_{j \< i} d(a, L\_j))$.  
3. **Result**: A set of $k$ (e.g., $k=100$) landmarks that are maximally spread out, defining the boundaries of the "Known Universe."

#### **5.1.2 The Deterministic Projection Algorithm**

When a new Atom $A$ enters the system (or needs refinement), its coordinates are calculated deterministically relative to the landmarks.

1. **Distance Calculation**: The Cortex calculates the squared distance vector $\\Delta\_A$ between the Atom $A$ and the $k$ landmarks. This distance is derived from the *graph distance* (number of hops in Compositions) or *statistical distance* (co-occurrence probability).  
   * $\\Delta\_A \= \[\\delta\_1^2, \\delta\_2^2, \\dots, \\delta\_k^2\]^T$.  
2. **Barycentric Placement**: The coordinate vector $\\vec{x}\_A$ in the target embedding space (XYZ) is computed via linear mapping:  
   * $\\vec{x}\_A \= \-\\frac{1}{2} L^{\\\#} (\\Delta\_A \- \\delta\_\\mu)$  
   * Where $L^{\\\#}$ is the pseudo-inverse of the landmark configuration matrix (pre-computed via Singular Value Decomposition on the landmarks).  
   * $\\delta\_\\mu$ is the mean squared distance of the landmarks from the centroid.

This process is strictly deterministic. If the same data is ingested twice, it maps to the exact same XYZ coordinates. There is no "random seed" drift.2

### **5.2 Law 2: Orthogonality via Modified Gram-Schmidt (MGS)**

A major logic error in early vector systems is **Axis Correlation**. If the X-axis represents "Size" and the Y-axis represents "Volume," they are highly correlated. The data will form a diagonal line in the spatial index.

* **The Index Consequence**: Spatial Indexes (R-Trees/GiST) rely on bounding boxes. If data lies on a diagonal, the bounding boxes of adjacent nodes overlap significantly. This degrades query performance from $O(\\log N)$ to $O(N)$, as the search cannot effectively prune branches.

To guarantee index efficiency, the Cortex applies **Modified Gram-Schmidt (MGS)** to the basis vectors of the semantic space.1

#### **5.2.1 The MGS Process**

After the initial MDS projection of the landmarks yields a basis set $\\{v\_1, v\_2, v\_3\\}$:

1. **Normalize First Axis**: $u\_1 \= \\frac{v\_1}{\\|v\_1\\|}$.  
2. **Orthogonalize Subsequent Axes**: For each subsequent axis $i$, project it onto the preceding orthonormal vectors and subtract the projection **iteratively**.  
   * $w\_i \= v\_i \- \\text{proj}\_{u\_1}(v\_i)$  
   * $w\_i \= w\_i \- \\text{proj}\_{u\_2}(w\_i) \\dots$  
   * $u\_i \= \\frac{w\_i}{\\|w\_i\\|}$  
3. **Why Modified?**: Classical Gram-Schmidt subtracts all projections from the original vector in one go. In finite-precision arithmetic (floating point), this leads to a loss of orthogonality due to cancellation errors. MGS subtracts errors iteratively, ensuring that the resulting axes are truly perpendicular (orthogonal) to machine precision.

#### **5.2.2 The Semantic Result**

This forces the semantic dimensions to be independent.

* **X (Principal 1\)**: Captures the primary variance (e.g., Abstract vs. Concrete).  
* **Y (Principal 2\)**: Captures secondary variance (e.g., Positive vs. Negative) independent of X.  
* **Index Efficiency**: The bounding boxes in the GiST index become minimal, square, and non-overlapping. This maximizes the pruning power of ST\_DWithin queries.

### **5.3 The "Dreaming" Cycle: Continuous Refinement**

The Cortex operates in a continuous loop, termed "Dreaming." This mimics memory consolidation in biological brains.

1. **Stress Monitoring**: The Cortex monitors the "Stress Function" of the system—the discrepancy between the *geometric distance* (Euclidean distance in geom) and the *observed relationship distance* (co-occurrences in new data).  
2. **Dirty Atom Selection**: It identifies Atoms with high stress (e.g., a new slang word that was placed randomly but is now showing strong usage patterns).  
3. **Recalibration**: It re-runs the LMDS projection for these atoms, pulling them to their correct semantic location.  
4. **Bulk Teleportation**: It executes a batch UPDATE via SPI to move the Atoms.

5. **Vacuum Integration**: Since PostgreSQL uses Multi-Version Concurrency Control (MVCC), updates create dead tuples. The Cortex coordinates with the Postgres Autovacuum daemon to clean up these dead tuples immediately, preventing bloat.

**6\. Inference: Spatial Traversal and Trajectory Matching**

In the Hartonomous system, we reject the notion that inference requires a separate runtime (like TensorFlow Serving). Inference is a database query. It is the act of traversing the spatial paths laid down by the Shader Pipeline.

### **6.1 Rejection of the Forward Pass**

In a neural network, inference is a forward pass: Input $\\rightarrow$ Layer 1 $\\rightarrow$ Layer 2 $\\rightarrow$ Output. This is computationally expensive ($O(Depth \\times Width^2)$) and rigid.  
In Hartonomous, inference is Spatial Path Intersection.

* **The Query**: A user query (e.g., "Predict the next word after 'The cat sat'") is shaded into a LINESTRING ZM trajectory.  
* **The Logic**: The system searches for existing **Memory Linestrings** (Compositions) that have a similar shape to the Query Linestring.  
* **The Prediction**: The endpoint of the best-matching Memory Linestring is the "inference result."

### **6.2 Geometric Similarity Metrics**

To find the "best match," we utilize specific spatial metrics provided by PostGIS. Standard Euclidean distance is insufficient because it only measures point-to-point proximity, ignoring the *shape* and *direction* of the thought.

#### **6.2.1 ST\_HausdorffDistance (The Coarse Filter)**

* **Definition**: The "maximum of the minimums." It measures the worst-case distance between any point on the Query Line and the closest point on the Memory Line.  
* **Usage**: This is a fast, coarse filter. It answers, "Are these two thoughts roughly in the same semantic neighborhood?"  
* **Limitation**: It is direction-agnostic. It cannot distinguish between "Cat eats Mouse" (A $\\rightarrow$ B) and "Mouse eats Cat" (B $\\rightarrow$ A).

#### **6.2.2 ST\_FréchetDistance (The Fine Filter)**

* **Definition**: The "Dog-Walking Distance." Imagine a person walking a dog. The person walks on the Query Line, the dog on the Memory Line. Both must move forward from start to end. The Fréchet distance is the minimum leash length required to traverse both curves simultaneously.  
* **Usage**: This is the **Gold Standard** for Hartonomous inference.1  
  * **Temporal Integrity**: Unlike Hausdorff, Fréchet distance respects the sequence of vertices. It ensures that the flow of the retrieved memory matches the flow of the query.  
  * **Causality**: It inherently enforces causal reasoning. A memory that matches the query spatially but in reverse chronological order will have a high Fréchet distance and be rejected.

### **6.3 The Inference Query Blueprint**

The "Inference Engine" is effectively a SQL query that utilizes these metrics.

SQL

\-- "Predict the next state based on the current trajectory"  
WITH query\_trajectory AS (  
    \-- The user's input shaded into a Linestring  
    SELECT ST\_GeomFromText('LINESTRING ZM(...)') AS geom  
)  
SELECT   
    memory.sdi\_id,  
    \-- Calculate similarity (Lower is better)  
    ST\_FrechetDistance(query\_trajectory.geom, memory.geom) AS similarity\_score,  
    \-- Retrieve the outcome (the end of the memory path)  
    ST\_EndPoint(memory.geom) AS predicted\_outcome  
FROM   
    atoms AS memory,  
    query\_trajectory  
WHERE   
    \-- 1\. Index Scan: Only look at memories in the same semantic region (GiST optimization)  
    memory.geom && ST\_Expand(query\_trajectory.geom, 0.5)  
      
    \-- 2\. Class Filter: We are looking for Compositions (patterns), not Constants  
    AND memory.class \= 'composition'  
      
    \-- 3\. Similarity Threshold: Must match the shape of the thought  
    AND ST\_FrechetDistance(query\_trajectory.geom, memory.geom) \< 0.2  
      
ORDER BY   
    \-- 4\. Prioritize 'Heavy' thoughts (High Frequency/Salience encoded in M)  
    \-- We sum the M values of the start and end points to find strong connections.  
    (ST\_M(ST\_StartPoint(memory.geom)) \+ ST\_M(ST\_EndPoint(memory.geom))) DESC  
LIMIT 1;

This query performs complex pattern matching and predictive inference entirely within the database kernel, utilizing the spatial index for logarithmic speed ($O(\\log N)$) instead of the linear speed of vector scans.

### **6.4 Attention as Spatial Range Queries**

Transformer models use "Attention Mechanisms" to focus on relevant parts of the context window. In Hartonomous, Attention is simply a **Range Query**.

* **Query**: "What concepts are related to 'Cat'?"  
* **Implementation**:

  WHERE ST\_3DDWithin(  
      geom,

**7\. Reconstruction and Recall: The Read Path**

A "Database-Centric" AI must be able to not just store atoms, but reconstruct reality from them. If a video file is decomposed into a million atoms, how do we retrieve it efficiently without crashing the client's memory?

### **7.1 The Streaming Reconstruction Challenge**

Standard SQL retrieval (SELECT data FROM...) is synchronous and buffers the entire result set in the server's RAM before sending it to the client. For reconstructing large files or long narratives, this causes massive memory spikes and unacceptable latency.

### **7.2 The PL/Python Streaming Solution**

Hartonomous utilizes **PL/Python** to implement a streaming interface. PL/Python allows direct manipulation of bytea streams and can utilize Python generators (yield) to stream results incrementally.1

#### **7.2.1 The reconstruct\_file Function**

This function takes a root SDI (the ID of the file concept) and streams the reconstructed content.

Python

CREATE OR REPLACE FUNCTION reconstruct\_file(root\_sdi BYTEA)  
RETURNS SETOF BYTEA AS $$  
    \# Prepare the query to traverse the geometric composition  
    \# We follow the 'composition' relationships (LineStrings) from the root  
    query \= """  
        SELECT child.data  
        FROM atoms root  
        JOIN atom\_relationships rel ON rel.parent\_sdi \= root.sdi\_id  
        JOIN atoms child ON child.sdi\_id \= rel.child\_sdi  
        WHERE root.sdi\_id \= $1    
        ORDER BY ST\_M(ST\_StartPoint(rel.geom)) ASC \-- Order by sequence (M)  
    """  
    plan \= plpy.prepare(query, \["bytea"\])  
      
    \# Open a cursor to avoid loading all chunks into RAM  
    cursor \= plpy.cursor(plan, \[root\_sdi\])  
      
    \# Stream the result in chunks  
    while True:  
        rows \= cursor.fetch(50) \# Fetch 50 atoms at a time  
        if not rows:  
            break  
        for row in rows:  
            yield row\['data'\] \# Stream raw binary to client  
$$ LANGUAGE plpython3u;

This effectively turns the database into a streaming media server. The Time-To-First-Byte (TTFB) is minimized because the function yields the first chunk as soon as the cursor fetches it, rather than waiting for the entire file to be reassembled.

**8\. Distillation and Pruning: The Art of Forgetting**

An infinite storage system eventually succumbs to entropy. A cognitive system must forget. In Hartonomous, forgetting is not an error; it is a maintenance process called **Distillation**.

### **8.1 The M-Threshold Policy**

The **Measure (M)** dimension encodes the "Global Salience" or "Frequency" of an Atom.

* **High M**: Core concepts, frequently accessed memories, strong neural pathways.  
* **Low M**: Noise, one-off events, fleeting thoughts.

The Cortex runs a background job (similar to Garbage Collection):

SQL

DELETE FROM atoms   
WHERE   
    class \= 'composition'   
    AND ST\_M(ST\_EndPoint(geom)) \< threshold   
    AND last\_accessed \< NOW() \- INTERVAL '1 month';

This prunes weak connections while preserving the strong "backbone" of knowledge.

### **8.2 Generalization via Geometric Merging**

Before deleting low-M atoms, the Cortex can attempt to "Generalize" them.

* **Scenario**: The system has 1000 memories of "eating a specific apple." They are all low-M specific events clustered tightly in space.  
* **Action**: The Cortex calculates the **Centroid** of these 1000 Linestrings.  
* **Result**: It creates a single new Archetype Atom: "Eating an Apple." This new Atom has a High M (sum of the constituents) and represents the *general concept* rather than the specific instances.  
* **Pruning**: The 1000 specific instances are then deleted. The system has "learned a lesson" and forgotten the details.

**9\. Hardware and Operational Realities**

The Hartonomous architecture necessitates a shift in hardware provisioning.

### **9.1 The Rejection of the GPU**

This system explicitly rejects the GPU. The workload is dominated by **Spatial Joins**, **Index Lookups**, and **Pointer Chasing**—tasks that are antagonistic to the SIMD (Single Instruction, Multiple Data) architecture of GPUs.

* **CPU**: We require High Core-Count CPUs (e.g., AMD EPYC Threadripper). Each core can handle a separate spatial query thread.  
* **Parallelism**: PostGIS 3.0+ supports parallel query execution. A 64-core CPU can execute a massive spatial join across 64 shards of the Hilbert index simultaneously.

### **9.2 Memory Hierarchy**

* **RAM**: Massive RAM is mandatory (512GB+). The goal is to fit the entire **GiST Index** and the **Hilbert Index** into PostgreSQL's shared\_buffers. If the index is in RAM, traversal is nanosecond-fast.  
* **Storage**: NVMe SSDs are required for the heap. While the index is in RAM, the actual payload (TOAST data) is on disk. Random read performance (IOPS) is the limiting factor for Reconstruction.

### **9.3 The "Copy-on-Write" Evolution**

To maintain stability, the system prefers **Immutability**.

* **Update Policy**: Once an Atom is placed and indexed, it acts as a reference point for others. Moving it is expensive (requires updating all parents).  
* **Drift Handling**: If a concept's meaning changes drastically, we prefer to create a *new* Atom with the new meaning (and a new timestamp/version in Z/M) rather than moving the old one. This preserves the historical context of the "old truth."
